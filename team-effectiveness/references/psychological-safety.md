# Psychological Safety

A deep dive into creating and maintaining psychological safety in teams.

## What Is Psychological Safety

### Definition

Psychological safety is a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking.

**Key aspects:**

- It's a team-level phenomenon, not individual
- It's about perception and belief, not objective reality
- It enables risk-taking, vulnerability, and learning

### What It Is and Isn't

| Psychological Safety IS | Psychological Safety IS NOT |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Feeling safe to speak up | Lack of accountability |
| Willingness to disagree | Avoiding conflict |
| Comfort admitting mistakes | Lowering standards |
| Asking "dumb" questions | Being nice all the time |
| Giving honest feedback | Saying whatever you want |
| Experimenting and failing | Reckless risk-taking |

**Critical distinction:** Psychological safety + accountability = High performance

Low safety + high accountability = Anxiety zone
High safety + low accountability = Comfort zone
High safety + high accountability = Learning zone (target)

## The Research Behind It

### Google's Project Aristotle

Google studied 180+ teams to identify what made teams effective. Results:

#### #1 predictor of team effectiveness: Psychological safety

Other factors (in order):
2. Dependability
3. Structure & Clarity
4. Meaning
5. Impact

**Key finding:** Who is on the team matters less than how the team works together.

### Amy Edmondson's Research

Harvard professor Amy Edmondson coined the term and has studied it for 20+ years.

**Key findings:**

- Teams with higher safety report more errors (not because they make more - they report more)
- High safety correlates with learning, innovation, and performance
- Safety can be built through deliberate leader behavior

### The Performance Paradox

Teams that feel safe to admit errors actually have fewer errors over time because:

- Problems are caught early
- Root causes are addressed
- Knowledge is shared
- Near-misses become learning opportunities

## Measuring Psychological Safety

### The Seven Questions

Amy Edmondson's team survey:

1. If you make a mistake on this team, it is often held against you. (R)
2. Members of this team are able to bring up problems and tough issues.
3. People on this team sometimes reject others for being different. (R)
4. It is safe to take a risk on this team.
5. It is difficult to ask other members of this team for help. (R)
6. No one on this team would deliberately act in a way that undermines my efforts.
7. Working with members of this team, my unique skills and talents are valued and utilized.

(R) = reverse scored

**Scoring:** 1-5 scale, higher = more safety. Team average matters.

### Behavioral Indicators

**High safety signs:**

- Questions asked in meetings
- Mistakes shared openly
- Ideas from all levels
- Healthy debate
- Help requested freely

**Low safety signs:**

- Silence in meetings
- Errors hidden
- Only leaders speak
- Quick consensus
- Suffering in silence

## Building Psychological Safety

### Leader Behaviors

#### 1. Frame the work

Position the work as a learning problem, not an execution problem.

"We're entering new territory. We'll need everyone's input to figure this out."

#### 2. Acknowledge your fallibility

Leaders who admit mistakes model that it's safe to do so.

"I don't have all the answers. I need your help."
"I made a mistake when I..."

#### 3. Ask good questions

Show genuine curiosity about others' perspectives.

"What are you seeing that I might be missing?"
"What concerns do you have?"
"What would you do differently?"

#### 4. Create structures for input

Don't rely on spontaneous speaking up - build it into processes.

- Round-robins in meetings
- Anonymous input channels
- Pre-meeting preparation
- Retrospectives

#### 5. Respond productively

How you respond to input determines if people will share again.

| When Someone Shares | Do This | Not This |
| ------------------- | ------- | -------- |
| An idea | "Tell me more" | "That won't work" |
| A problem | "Thanks for raising it" | "Why didn't you say earlier?" |
| A mistake | "What did we learn?" | "How could you let this happen?" |
| A question | Answer without judgment | "You should know this" |
| Disagreement | "Help me understand" | Dismiss or ignore |

### Team Member Contributions

You don't need to be a leader to build safety.

#### 1. Model vulnerability

Share your own struggles, questions, mistakes.

#### 2. Support risk-takers

When someone speaks up, respond positively even if you disagree with the content.

#### 3. Ask clarifying questions

Normalize that not everyone understands everything.

#### 4. Give credit

Highlight others' contributions, especially in front of leadership.

#### 5. Address exclusion

Notice when someone is talked over, ignored, or dismissed.

## Repairing Psychological Safety

### After a Safety Violation

When safety has been damaged:

#### 1. Acknowledge the damage

"I realize my reaction last time may have made it harder to share concerns. I'm sorry."

#### 2. Explain the context (not excuse)

"I was stressed about the deadline, but that's not an excuse for how I responded."

#### 3. Reaffirm the value

"Your input is important to me and the team. I want to hear your concerns."

#### 4. Create a safe test

Provide an opportunity for the person to take a small risk with guaranteed positive response.

### Rebuilding Trust

Trust builds slowly through consistent behavior over time.

**The trust equation:**

```text
Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation
```

**Rebuilding actions:**

- Keep small commitments
- Follow through on promises
- Show consistent behavior
- Respond well to vulnerable moments
- Prioritize team over personal agenda

## Psychological Safety Across Cultures

### Cultural Considerations

Different cultures have different baseline norms around:

- Hierarchy and deference to authority
- Direct vs. indirect communication
- Individual vs. collective identity
- Comfort with uncertainty

**Implications:**

- What feels "safe" varies across cultures
- Don't assume your comfort zone is universal
- Create multiple channels for input
- Watch for cultural "tax" on underrepresented groups

### Adapting Your Approach

| Cultural Dimension | Adaptation |
| ------------------ | ---------- |
| High power distance | Private input channels, anonymous options |
| Indirect communication | Read between lines, follow up 1:1 |
| Collectivist | Group consensus building, save face |
| High uncertainty avoidance | More structure, clearer expectations |

## Common Misconceptions

### "We're already safe - no one complains"

Silence is often a sign of low safety, not high. People in unsafe environments learn not to speak up.

### "I have an open door policy"

Inviting input isn't enough. You have to actively seek it and respond well to it.

### "We can't have safety AND accountability"

You need both. High safety without accountability = comfort zone. High accountability without safety = anxiety zone.

### "It's just about being nice"

Psychological safety means people can give you hard feedback without fear. Nice and honest aren't opposites.

### "Some people just won't speak up"

Almost everyone will contribute if the environment is safe enough. Low participation usually indicates low safety.

## Exercises for Teams

### Personal History Exercise (60 min)

Build trust through personal sharing.

- Each person shares something about their background
- Where they grew up, family, early work experiences
- Focus on humanizing, not problem-solving

### Failure Résumé (45 min)

Normalize failure as part of learning.

- Each person shares a professional failure and what they learned
- Leaders go first
- Celebrate the learning, not just the story

### Start-Stop-Continue (30 min)

Retrospective format that invites honest feedback.

- What should we start doing?
- What should we stop doing?
- What should we continue doing?

### Pre-mortem (30 min)

Imagine a project has failed. What went wrong?

- Surfaces concerns in a safe way
- Treats problems as hypothetical
- Leads to prevention rather than blame
