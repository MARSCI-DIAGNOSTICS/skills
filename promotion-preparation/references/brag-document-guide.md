# Brag Document Guide

A comprehensive guide to creating and maintaining an effective brag document (work log, wins journal).

## What is a Brag Document?

A brag document is your personal, ongoing record of accomplishments, contributions, and wins. It's called a "brag document" but it's really a factual record - not bragging, just documenting.

**Alternative Names:**

- Work log
- Wins journal
- Accomplishments tracker
- Career diary
- Impact log

## Why Keep a Brag Document?

### Memory is Unreliable

Research shows we forget 80% of what we did within weeks. By review time, you'll remember recent work vividly but struggle to recall earlier contributions.

### Recency Bias is Real

Both you and your manager are subject to recency bias. The last month looms larger than the previous eleven. Regular tracking prevents this distortion.

### Small Wins Add Up

Individual small wins feel insignificant, but patterns emerge over time:

- "Fixed 3 bugs" × 12 months = "Resolved 36 bugs, reducing error rate by 40%"
- "Helped teammate" × 50 = "Served as go-to mentor for 5 engineers"

### Confidence Building

On hard days, reviewing wins combats imposter syndrome. Seeing documented evidence of your value is powerful.

### Interview Preparation

Your brag document becomes a goldmine of STAR stories for future interviews. Ready-made examples with specific details.

Spend 5-10 minutes each Friday capturing the week's wins.


## What to Document

### Always Capture

**Projects and Features:**

- What you shipped
- Your specific contributions
- Timeline and complexity
- Outcomes and metrics

**Problem Solving:**

- Issues you debugged
- Incidents you resolved
- Optimizations you made
- Technical debt you addressed

**Collaboration:**

- People you helped
- Teams you worked with
- Meetings you led
- Consensus you built

**Learning:**

- New technologies learned
- Skills developed
- Training completed
- Books/courses finished

**Recognition:**

- Positive feedback (copy exact quotes)
- Awards or shoutouts
- Thanks from teammates
- Kudos in public channels

### Often Overlooked (But Important)

**"Glue Work":**

- Onboarding new teammates
- Improving documentation
- Streamlining processes
- Organizing team events
- Filling gaps no one else noticed

**Prevention:**

- Problems you prevented (hard to see!)
- Technical choices that avoided issues
- Warnings you raised that were heeded

**Context & Expertise:**

- Questions you answered
- Being the "go-to person" for X
- Tribal knowledge you documented
- Decisions you informed

## Writing Effective Entries

### The STAR Format for Entries

**Situation:** Brief context
**Task:** What you needed to do
**Action:** What you specifically did
**Result:** Outcome with metrics

**Example:**

```markdown
## Fixed Critical Payment Bug (October 2024)

**Situation:** Payments were failing intermittently, affecting ~2%
of transactions. Issue had been open for 2 weeks.

**Task:** Identify and fix the root cause.

**Action:** Analyzed logs, identified race condition in payment
confirmation, implemented distributed lock solution, added
comprehensive tests, documented fix and prevention.

**Result:** Zero payment failures since fix (2+ months).
Estimated $50K/month in previously lost transactions recovered.
```

### Quantification Tips

**Time:**

- "Reduced build time from 20 minutes to 5 minutes"
- "Saved 2 hours/week of manual work"
- "Completed 2 weeks ahead of schedule"

**Money:**

- "Reduced infrastructure costs by $10K/month"
- "Prevented potential $100K outage"
- "Enabled $500K deal with new feature"

**Scale:**

- "Serving 1M requests/day"
- "Used by 50 engineers daily"
- "Impacted 10K customers"

**Improvement:**

- "Reduced errors by 80%"
- "Improved latency by 5x"
- "Increased coverage from 40% to 85%"

### If You Can't Quantify

Use qualitative evidence:

- Direct quotes from feedback
- Before/after comparisons
- Testimonials from users/teammates
- Comparison to previous approach

## Common Mistakes

### Being Too Modest

**Wrong approach:** Only log "big" wins
**Right approach:** Log everything, filter later

You're a poor judge of what's impressive. Small things add up. When in doubt, write it down.

### Waiting Too Long

**Wrong approach:** "I'll catch up at the end of the quarter"
**Right approach:** 5 minutes weekly, 30 minutes monthly

Fresh memories have details. Stale memories lack context. Future-you won't remember.

### No Context

**Wrong approach:** "Fixed the bug"
**Right approach:** "Fixed payment retry bug that was causing 2% of transactions to fail, saving estimated $50K/month"

Include enough context that you can reconstruct the story 6 months later.

### Only Technical Wins

**Wrong approach:** Only coding accomplishments
**Right approach:** Also capture collaboration, mentoring, process improvements, "glue work"

Soft skills matter for promotion. Document all types of contributions.

### Private Only

**Wrong approach:** Keep everything private
**Right approach:** Share selectively with manager

Your brag document can inform 1:1s, status updates, and review discussions. It's not secret.

## Making It Sustainable

### Make It Easy

- Use a simple format you'll actually use
- Keep it where you'll see it (doc, wiki, notes app)
- Set a recurring calendar reminder
- Link it from your daily workflow

### Start Small

- Begin with just weekly bullets
- Add monthly synthesis when comfortable
- Expand format as you see value
- Don't aim for perfection

### Review Regularly

- Read past entries before 1:1s
- Use it for status updates
- Reference it in performance reviews
- Let it inform your development plan

### Share Appropriately

With your manager:

- Use entries to inform 1:1 discussions
- Share the document before reviews
- Reference specific entries when discussing promotion

With yourself:

- Read on hard days for confidence
- Use for interview prep
- Reference when updating resume
