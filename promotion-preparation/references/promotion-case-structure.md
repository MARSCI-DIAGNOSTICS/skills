# Promotion Case Structure Reference

A complete guide to building compelling promotion case documents.

## The Three-Section Framework

### Section 1: Accomplishments & Impact

This is the heart of your case. Document specific achievements with measurable outcomes.

**Structure for Each Accomplishment:**

```markdown
## [Accomplishment Title]

**What I Did:** [Brief description of the work]

**How I Did It:** [Your approach, skills used, challenges overcome]

**Impact:** [Quantified outcomes, business value, downstream effects]

**Skills Demonstrated:** [Competencies this shows]
```

**Example:**

```markdown
## Redesigned Payment Processing Pipeline

**What I Did:** Architected and led implementation of a new payment
processing system to replace our legacy synchronous pipeline.

**How I Did It:** Conducted analysis of existing bottlenecks, designed
event-driven architecture using Kafka, led team of 3 engineers over
4 months, established monitoring and alerting, created runbooks.

**Impact:**
- Reduced payment processing latency from 2s to 200ms (90% reduction)
- Increased throughput from 100 TPS to 2000 TPS
- Enabled Black Friday traffic without manual intervention
- Eliminated weekend on-call escalations (was 2-3/month)

**Skills Demonstrated:** System design, technical leadership,
cross-team collaboration (worked with payments, infrastructure, SRE)
```

### Section 2: Growth & Learning

Show trajectory, not just current state. Demonstrate that you're continuously improving.

**Categories to Cover:**

**Skills Developed:**

- Technical skills deepened or acquired
- Soft skills improved
- Domain knowledge expanded

**Challenges Overcome:**

- Difficult situations handled well
- Mistakes made and lessons learned
- Adversity faced and resolved

**Feedback Incorporated:**

- Specific feedback received
- Actions taken in response
- Results of those changes

**Example:**

```markdown
## Growth & Learning

### Skills Developed

**System Design:** Progressed from implementing designs to leading
design discussions. Now regularly consulted on architecture decisions
across the team.

**Mentoring:** Developed structured approach to onboarding. Both
engineers I mentored were promoted within 12 months.

### Challenges Overcome

**Q2 Incident:** Led response to our largest outage (4 hours).
Established incident command, coordinated 12 engineers, wrote
post-mortem that led to 3 systemic improvements. Manager feedback:
"Showed leadership under pressure."

### Feedback Incorporated

**Previous Review:** Manager noted I "sometimes moved too fast without
bringing the team along."

**Actions Taken:** Started writing design docs before implementation,
holding design reviews, explicitly seeking input from quieter team
members.

**Results:** Recent 360 feedback highlighted improved collaboration.
No recent concerns about pace.
```

### Section 3: Future Focus

Demonstrate readiness for next level by showing forward-thinking vision.

**Elements to Include:**

**Goals Aligned to Next Level:**

- What you'll focus on if promoted
- How this aligns with team/org needs
- Why you're the right person

**Larger Scope Ready to Take On:**

- Specific opportunities you're prepared for
- Evidence you can handle increased scope
- Transition plan for current responsibilities

**Development Areas:**

- Honest assessment of gaps
- Plan to address them
- Progress already made

**Example:**

```markdown
## Future Focus

### Goals for Staff Level

If promoted, I plan to focus on:

1. **Technical Strategy:** Lead development of our 3-year technical
   roadmap for the payments domain, aligning with business goals.

2. **Engineering Excellence:** Establish code review standards and
   mentoring program to raise team quality bar.

3. **Cross-org Influence:** Represent payments engineering in
   architectural decisions affecting the platform.

### Scope I'm Ready For

- **Multi-team Projects:** Successfully led payment-infra-SRE
  collaboration; ready for larger cross-team initiatives
- **Technical Decision-Making:** Already making architecture
  decisions for my team; ready for org-wide impact
- **Mentoring Senior Engineers:** Currently mentoring L4s; ready
  to help L5s grow

### Development Areas

**Area:** Executive communication
**Plan:** Enrolled in technical writing course, practicing monthly
business reviews with manager feedback
**Progress:** Last all-hands presentation received positive feedback
from VP Engineering
```

## Tips for Strong Promotion Cases

### Quantify Everything Possible

**Weak:** "Improved system performance"

**Strong:** "Reduced P99 latency from 2.1s to 340ms, enabling 3x traffic growth"

Types of metrics:

- Time saved (hours/week, days/project)
- Money saved or generated
- Percentage improvements
- Scale (users, requests, transactions)
- Frequency reduction (incidents, bugs, escalations)

### Use the "So What?" Test

For every accomplishment, ask "So what?" until you reach business impact.

- "I refactored the authentication module"
- So what?
- "It reduced bugs in that area"
- So what?
- "We had zero auth-related incidents for 6 months"
- So what?
- "This saved the on-call team ~4 hours/month and prevented potential revenue loss from auth outages"

### Show, Don't Tell

**Weak:** "I'm a strong technical leader"

**Strong:** "Led design and implementation of payment pipeline
(3 engineers, 4 months), mentored 2 engineers to promotion,
established code review standards now used team-wide"

### Address Gaps Proactively

Don't pretend weaknesses don't exist. Show awareness and action:

"While my public speaking has historically been a development area,
I've taken specific steps to improve: completed a presentation skills
workshop, volunteered for 3 team demos, and presented at the last
engineering all-hands. Feedback has been increasingly positive."

### Tailor to Your Company

- Use your company's level expectations as a checklist
- Reference specific competencies from your company's framework
- Align language with how your company talks about these levels
- Include examples relevant to your company's priorities
