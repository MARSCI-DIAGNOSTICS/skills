# Manager's Perspective Reference

Understanding how managers think about promotions helps you build a stronger case and work more effectively toward advancement.

## How Managers View Promotions

### What Managers Need

When your manager advocates for your promotion, they need:

#### Evidence to Present

- Concrete accomplishments with measurable impact
- Specific examples for each competency
- Supporting artifacts (docs, metrics, feedback)
- Clear narrative they can articulate

#### Confidence in Success

- Belief you'll succeed at the next level
- Evidence you're already operating at that level
- Pattern of consistent performance
- Absence of concerning gaps

#### Reduced Risk

- "Safe bet" rather than "stretch" promotion
- No significant concerns to address
- History of reliable delivery
- Ability to handle increased scope

#### Peer Support

- Positive peer feedback
- Recognition from cross-functional partners
- Respect from team members
- No concerning interpersonal issues

### What Makes Your Manager's Job Easy

#### Well-Documented Accomplishments

- You track and can articulate your wins
- Impact is quantified where possible
- Context is preserved
- Evidence is readily available

#### Already Operating at Next Level

- You're doing the job before getting the title
- Examples span all competency areas
- Scope has naturally expanded
- Others already treat you as that level

#### Clear Narrative

- Your story is easy to tell
- Growth trajectory is visible
- Path to promotion is obvious
- No need to make arguments

#### Proactive Communication

- You surface wins appropriately
- You discuss gaps and development
- You align on expectations
- You don't wait to be asked

### What Makes Your Manager's Job Hard

#### Vague Contributions

- "Good team player" without specifics
- Technical work without business impact
- Invisible contributions
- No documented evidence

#### Concerning Gaps

- Missing critical competencies
- Feedback not being addressed
- Soft skill concerns
- Consistency issues

#### Unclear Narrative

- Hard to articulate why you should be promoted
- Accomplishments don't tell a story
- Mixed signals about readiness
- Questions they can't answer

#### Poor Timing

- Asking when budget is constrained
- Not enough time at current level
- Recent performance concerns
- Organizational uncertainty

## The Promotion Process (Manager's View)

### Before the Discussion

What managers do before promotion cycles:

1. **Review level expectations** - What does next level require?
2. **Assess your work** - Do you meet those expectations?
3. **Gather evidence** - What supports the case?
4. **Identify concerns** - What might be raised against?
5. **Plan the narrative** - What story will they tell?

### During Calibration

What happens when managers meet to discuss promotions:

1. **Each manager presents** their candidates
2. **Evidence is scrutinized** by peers
3. **Concerns are raised** and addressed
4. **Comparison across candidates** happens
5. **Budget constraints** are applied
6. **Decisions are made** or deferred

**Key insight:** Your manager isn't the only decision-maker. They need to convince their peers and their manager.

### What Gets Discussed

Questions that come up in calibration:

- "What specifically did they accomplish?"
- "How do we know they're ready for more scope?"
- "Are there any concerns about [competency]?"
- "How do they compare to others at this level?"
- "What would happen if we wait another cycle?"
- "Is this a 'must promote' or 'could promote'?"

## Working Effectively with Your Manager

### Alignment is Critical

**What you need from your manager:**

- Clear understanding of next-level expectations
- Honest assessment of where you are
- Specific feedback on gaps
- Opportunities to demonstrate readiness
- Advocacy when you're ready

**What your manager needs from you:**

- Documented accomplishments
- Proactive communication
- Gap awareness and development
- Patience and professionalism
- Making their job easy

### How to Align

**Early in the Cycle (6+ months before review):**

Ask in a 1:1:

- "I'm interested in growing toward [next level]. What does that look like here?"
- "Where am I relative to next-level expectations?"
- "What gaps do you see?"
- "What opportunities exist to demonstrate readiness?"

**Ongoing:**

- Share wins regularly (without being annoying)
- Discuss progress on development areas
- Ask for feedback on stretch assignments
- Show awareness of next-level expectations

**Before Review:**

- Share your promotion case draft
- Ask for honest feedback
- Discuss timing and readiness
- Understand the process and timeline

### What to Ask for Feedback

**Good questions:**

- "What would make my promotion case stronger?"
- "If you were presenting my case, what would you highlight?"
- "What concerns might come up in calibration?"
- "How do I compare to others who were promoted to [level]?"

**Avoid:**

- "Do you think I deserve a promotion?" (puts them on the spot)
- "Why haven't I been promoted yet?" (sounds entitled)
- "Is there anything wrong with my work?" (too vague)

## Common Concerns Managers Have

Understanding potential concerns helps you address them proactively.

### Technical Concerns

#### "Not deep enough"

- Breadth without depth
- Surface-level knowledge
- Relies too much on others

**Address by:** Deep dive on a complex problem, write technical content, become the expert in an area

#### "Quality issues"

- Too many bugs
- Code review feedback patterns
- Incidents caused

**Address by:** Improve testing, slow down, seek review before shipping

### Leadership Concerns

#### "Doesn't mentor effectively"

- Junior team members not growing
- Hoards knowledge
- Poor feedback skills

**Address by:** Actively mentor, document what you teach, seek feedback on your mentoring

#### "Not visible enough"

- Great work no one knows about
- Doesn't present or share
- Stays in the background

**Address by:** Present at team meetings, write and share documents, demo your work

### Collaboration Concerns

#### "Difficult to work with"

- Peer feedback is lukewarm
- Cross-team friction
- Communication challenges

**Address by:** Seek peer feedback, improve relationships, work on communication

#### "Works in isolation"

- Doesn't seek input
- Makes decisions alone
- Not a team player

**Address by:** Involve others early, document decisions, collaborate publicly

### Consistency Concerns

#### "Inconsistent performance"

- Great quarters and bad quarters
- Unreliable delivery
- Variable quality

**Address by:** Establish sustainable pace, build systems, address root causes

#### "Not enough time at level"

- Just promoted recently
- Need to demonstrate consistency
- Pattern not established

**Address by:** Be patient, continue building evidence, show sustained performance

## What Managers Look For by Level

### Mid → Senior

Managers want to see:

- Can own significant features end-to-end
- Mentors junior engineers naturally
- Makes sound technical decisions
- Communicates effectively with stakeholders
- Improves team processes/practices

### Senior → Staff

Managers want to see:

- Impact beyond immediate team
- Influences technical direction
- Develops other senior engineers
- Navigates organizational complexity
- Creates leverage (multiplier effect)

### Staff → Principal

Managers want to see:

- Company-wide technical impact
- Shapes engineering culture
- External visibility/reputation
- Strategic influence
- Develops staff engineers

## The Review Process

### What Managers Write

In performance reviews, managers typically document:

**Summary statement:** Overall assessment of performance
**Accomplishments:** Key wins with impact
**Competency ratings:** Assessment against each area
**Development areas:** Where growth is needed
**Recommendation:** Promote, on track, needs improvement

### How to Help

**Before review:**

- Provide your brag document
- Share your self-assessment
- Highlight specific accomplishments
- Include supporting evidence

**Make it easy to write:**

- Organized, specific examples
- Quantified impact
- Clear connection to competencies
- Evidence links included

## If Promotion Doesn't Happen

### Common Reasons

**Legitimate reasons:**

- Genuine gaps in competencies
- Not enough time at level
- Budget constraints
- Organizational changes

**Addressable reasons:**

- Visibility problem (work not seen)
- Documentation problem (not captured)
- Communication problem (not articulated)
- Timing problem (asked too early)

### How to Respond

**In the moment:**

- Listen without defensiveness
- Ask clarifying questions
- Understand the specific gaps
- Request actionable feedback

**After the conversation:**

- Process emotions privately
- Create a development plan
- Align with manager on goals
- Set check-in timeline

**Moving forward:**

- Address feedback genuinely
- Track progress visibly
- Stay engaged and professional
- Consider if this is the right place

### Questions to Ask

- "What specifically would change the outcome next cycle?"
- "What would 'ready for promotion' look like?"
- "What opportunities exist to demonstrate growth?"
- "How can I get more visibility for my work?"
- "What feedback from peers or stakeholders should I address?"
